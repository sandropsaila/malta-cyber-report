Malta Gaming Authority (MGA) — System Breach
Critical March 2026 iGaming / Regulatory

On 17 March 2026, the Malta Gaming Authority confirmed a breach of one of its internal systems, immediately activating incident response protocols. The authority declined to identify the attacker or detail the extent of data compromised.

Days later, German security researcher Lilith Wittmann claimed responsibility via X and LinkedIn. She addressed the MGA: "Yes, I hacked you, and the data obtained has been shared with media partners, authorities." She alleged the MGA enabled "organised crime enablement schemes" and warned any extradition attempt would trigger release of her full iGaming data archive.

Wittmann stated she had maintained access to MGA systems for months before detection. The MGA called the allegations "unsubstantiated." Maltese authorities opened a criminal investigation; Wittmann faces up to 10 years imprisonment under Maltese law.

Fast Track (Malta-based CRM) — Third-Party Casino Data Breach
High October 2025 iGaming / CRM

Fast Track, a Malta-headquartered iGaming CRM platform serving over 100 casino operators worldwide, detected a cyberattack in early October 2025. Two operators were confirmed impacted; Shuffle Casino was the only publicly identified victim.

The breach exposed full names, email and home addresses, phone numbers, transaction histories, betting patterns, and support logs. Most critically, attackers obtained KYC verification documents including passport and driver's licence copies.

Fast Track had renewed its SOC 2 Type 2 accreditation just months earlier in June 2025. Shuffle announced it would end its partnership with Fast Track and explore alternative providers with reduced third-party risk.

The Mill Adventure (Malta) — API Flaw Exposes 800K+ Players
High March 2025 iGaming / Software Provider

The Mill Adventure, a Malta-based gaming platform provider, had a critically vulnerable unauthenticated GraphQL API allowing anyone to read player records without credentials. Researcher Lilith Wittmann disclosed the flaw on 14 March 2025.

The breach impacted users of Merkur's German platforms — Slotmagie, Crazybuzzer, and Merkurbets — exposing player IDs, names, addresses, IBANs, login records, and over 70,000 identity verification documents. Up to 800,000 players were affected.

Germany's regulator (GGL) issued a public reprimand on 17 March 2025, placing The Mill Adventure and its Malta-based subsidiaries on a public warnings registry. Over 12 unlicensed casino sites subsequently went offline.

Saint James Hospital Group — Sophisticated Cyberattack
High April 2025 Healthcare

On 18 April 2025, Malta's Saint James Hospital Group — operating hospitals in Sliema and Żejtun — confirmed it was the target of a sophisticated cyberattack. Upon detection, containment protocols were immediately activated and all systems physically isolated from external networks.

The hospital's IT, legal, and security teams, supported by external cybersecurity experts, launched a comprehensive forensic investigation to determine whether any sensitive patient data had been accessed. All medical appointments and procedures continued as planned.

An initial notification was submitted to the competent regulatory authorities and the police were formally informed. The hospital published updates on its website and set up a dedicated helpline for affected stakeholders.

Malta Tax & Customs Administration (MTCA) — Email Data Leak
Medium November 2025 Government / Tax

On 12 November 2025, the Malta Tax and Customs Administration confirmed a data breach caused by a procedural failure rather than a cyberattack. During a routine outbound communication, a file containing company contact details was accidentally attached to mass emails instead of being distributed through the established secure portal.

The file distributed contained company names, registration numbers, tax identification numbers, statuses, email addresses, and phone numbers of approximately 7,000 businesses. No financial data or taxpayer records were included.

The IDPC was formally notified in line with GDPR obligations, and an internal review was launched to identify contributing factors and introduce additional safeguards. The Opposition PN party called for an independent investigation and ministerial accountability.

Malta Security Service — Reported Wave of Public Sector & SME Attacks
High 2023–2024 Government / SMEs / National Security

The Malta Security Service (MSS) 2023 and 2024 annual reports — tabled in Parliament in 2025 — confirmed that Malta experienced "a number of cyber incidents" during its high-profile tenure on the UN Security Council (2023–2024) and its 2024 chairmanship of the OSCE.

Incidents included phishing attempts aimed at public sector employees, ransomware attacks on SMEs, and DDoS attacks intended to disrupt online services. The MSS found no direct link between Malta's diplomatic engagements and the attacks, but noted Malta's higher geopolitical visibility likely elevated its cyber-risk profile.

Most attacks were assessed as financially motivated or aimed at service disruption rather than espionage. The MSS stressed that secure communication channels are essential to protecting Malta's credibility in international forums.

Transport Malta — Server & IP Systems Breach
High September 2020 Government / Transport

Between the nights of 25–26 September 2020, Transport Malta suffered a cyberattack that shut down its online systems for five days. The authority's servers and intellectual property systems were compromised.

Employees were subsequently informed of the breach and advised to take precautionary measures including monitoring personal accounts and reviewing card transactions for unauthorised activity. A data breach notification was submitted to the IDPC and a magisterial inquiry was opened, with a magistrate appointing forensic experts to preserve evidence.

Transport Malta did not publicly confirm what personal data — if any — was exfiltrated. The attack disrupted online services used by both the public and the transport sector.

C-Planet IT Solutions — Maltese Voter Database Leak
Critical 2020 (Fined 2022) Political / Civil Society

In April 2020, a database held on C-Planet IT Solutions servers was found to contain the personally identifiable information of virtually every eligible voter in Malta's 2013 elections — over 330,000 individuals — including names, addresses, ID card numbers, dates of birth, and critically, political opinions and voting intentions.

The IDPC opened an investigation and found C-Planet had processed the data without a valid lawful basis, failed to implement appropriate security measures, and failed to notify the IDPC or affected individuals of the breach. In January 2022, C-Planet was fined €65,000 — at the time Malta's highest GDPR fine. The origin of the data remains disputed, with a Times of Malta report identifying the governing Labour Party as a possible source.

Privacy rights organisation noyb filed multiple complaints; a collective legal action was initiated by the Daphne Caruana Galizia Foundation and Repubblika. The case remains unresolved as to who originally compiled and shared the voter data.

Bank of Valletta (BOV) — €13M Nation-State Cyber Heist
Critical February 2019 Financial Services

On 13 February 2019, Malta's largest bank, Bank of Valletta, was forced to shut down all operations after the hacking group EmpireMonkey falsified €13 million in international transactions, transferring funds to accounts in the US, UK, Czech Republic, and Hong Kong. All branches, ATMs, mobile banking, and email services were suspended.

The bank detected the attack within 30 minutes during routine reconciliation of international transfers. Correspondent banks were contacted to block and reverse transactions, with €10.1 million ultimately recovered. The remaining €2.9 million was later located in Hong Kong with legal proceedings initiated.

A subsequent investigation revealed that EmpireMonkey had first targeted HSBC Malta as early as October 2018 — three months before the BOV heist — using malicious documents to compromise employees. The National Cyber Security Committee, police, MFSA, and Europol were all involved in the response.

FreeHour App — Vulnerability Disclosure & Legal Controversy
Medium 2022–2025 Tech / Education

In October 2022, four University of Malta computer science students discovered critical flaws in FreeHour — Malta's most popular student app used by over 90% of tertiary students. The vulnerabilities included unauthenticated API endpoints that exposed user data including phone numbers, email addresses, and class schedules.

Following standard ethical hacking protocols, the students notified FreeHour's founder Zach Ciappara and requested a standard bug bounty. Instead, FreeHour filed a criminal complaint. Students were arrested in November 2022, strip-searched, and had 14 devices impounded for 28 months. They faced up to 4 years imprisonment. Two defendants reported PTSD as a result.

The case triggered national debate, accelerated Malta's adoption of Budapest Convention Chapter III provisions in January 2025, and prompted the government to launch a public consultation on ethical hacker protections. On 1 July 2025, the Prime Minister announced a Presidential Pardon for all involved.

Broader Threat Landscape
Context · 2025–2026
🎯 Scale of Attacks

A PwC T-Pot honeypot deployment within Maltese IP ranges recorded over 6 million distinct attacks between August and October 2025 alone — more than one every second. The threat is constant, not episodic.

🔒 SME Blind Spots

A damning 2026 statistic: 20% of Maltese companies do not know whether they have been attacked. Malta's interconnected economy means a single weak vendor can cascade risk across multiple businesses.

🌐 iGaming as Primary Attack Surface

Malta's iGaming sector generates over €714M GVA per half-year (H1 2025), representing 6.5% of GDP. Five of the ten incidents in this report trace directly to the iGaming ecosystem — making it by far the highest-risk sector.

⚠ Third-Party Risk Pattern

The Fast Track, Mill Adventure, and BOV incidents all exploited trusted third-party platform providers or supply chain entry points rather than targeting end-operators directly. Vendor access management is now the single most critical control.

🏥 Healthcare Emerging Target

The Saint James Hospital attack in April 2025 marks the first confirmed cyberattack on a Maltese healthcare provider, consistent with a Europe-wide trend of hospitals being targeted for ransom and data theft due to the sensitivity of medical records.

⚖️ Legal Framework Gap

The FreeHour case exposed that Malta lacked legal protections for ethical hackers — researchers who responsibly disclose vulnerabilities faced criminal prosecution. This gap has now been partially addressed through the Budapest Convention and a Presidential Pardon, with further reform underway.
