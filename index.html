DetailsMalta Gaming Authority (MGA) — System Breach
CriticalMarch 2026iGaming / Regulatory

On 17 March 2026 the MGA confirmed a breach of an internal system. Days later, Berlin-based researcher Lilith Wittmann of the Chaos Computer Club claimed responsibility, stating data had been shared with media and authorities. She alleged the MGA enabled "organised crime enablement schemes" and warned any extradition attempt would trigger release of a full iGaming data archive.

Wittmann stated she maintained access for months before detection, calling the breach straightforward. The MGA called the allegations "unsubstantiated." Criminal investigation opened; Wittmann faces up to 10 years under Maltese law.

DetailsFast Track (Malta-based CRM) — Third-Party Casino Data Breach
HighOctober 2025iGaming / CRM

Fast Track, a Malta-headquartered iGaming CRM serving 100+ casino operators, was attacked in October 2025. Shuffle Casino was the only publicly confirmed victim. Attackers accessed full names, addresses, phone numbers, transaction histories, betting patterns, and critically, KYC documents including passports and driver's licences.

Fast Track held a fresh SOC 2 Type 2 certification (June 2025). Shuffle terminated the partnership. The breach exemplifies how third-party CRM providers are now prime iGaming attack vectors.

DetailsThe Mill Adventure (Malta) — API Flaw Exposes 800K+ Players
HighMarch 2025iGaming / Software

Malta-based gaming provider The Mill Adventure had a critically vulnerable unauthenticated GraphQL API exposing player records without credentials. Wittmann disclosed the flaw 14 March 2025. Affected: Merkur's German platforms — Slotmagie, Crazybuzzer, Merkurbets. Exposed: player IDs, names, IBANs, login records, and 70,000+ identity documents. ~800,000 players affected.

Germany's GGL issued a public reprimand; Malta-based subsidiaries placed on a warnings registry for failing mandatory annual pen tests. 12+ unlicensed casinos went offline.

DetailsMelita (Malta's Largest ISP) — DDoS Extortion Attack
HighJanuary 2021Telecoms / ISP

DetailsMalta Security Service — Reported Wave of Public Sector & SME Attacks
High2023–2024Government / SMEs / National Security

MSS annual reports (tabled 2025) confirmed Malta suffered a surge in cyber incidents during its UN Security Council tenure (2023–2024) and OSCE chairmanship. Incidents included phishing at public sector employees, ransomware on SMEs, and DDoS attacks. Most were financially motivated. No direct link to diplomatic activity established but elevated geopolitical profile likely raised risk.

DetailsC-Planet IT Solutions — Maltese Voter Database Leak
Critical2020 (Fined 2022)Political / Civil Society

In April 2020, a database on C-Planet servers exposed PII of 330,000+ Maltese voters (98% of the electorate), including names, ID card numbers, dates of birth, and political opinions and voting intentions. IDPC opened an investigation; in January 2022 C-Planet was fined €65,000 — Malta's highest GDPR fine at the time. noyb filed complaints; class action initiated. Origin of data remains disputed.

DetailsFreeHour App — Vulnerability Disclosure & Legal Controversy
Medium2022–2025Tech / Education

Four UoM students found critical flaws in FreeHour (Malta's most popular student app) — unauthenticated API endpoints exposing user data. Rather than recognition, students were arrested, strip-searched, and had 14 devices impounded for 28 months. Two reported PTSD. Case triggered national debate, Budapest Convention adoption (January 2025), and a Presidential Pardon (July 2025).

DetailsBank of Valletta (BOV) — €13M Nation-State Cyber Heist
CriticalFebruary 2019Financial Services

On 13 February 2019, EmpireMonkey APT transferred €13M from Malta's largest bank via falsified international transactions to accounts in the US, UK, Czech Republic, and Hong Kong. BOV shut down all operations within 30 minutes of detection — branches, ATMs, mobile banking, email. €10.1M recovered; remaining €2.9M located in Hong Kong. EmpireMonkey had previously targeted HSBC Malta (October 2018). 6 UK arrests followed.

DetailsLands Authority — Google-Indexed Personal Data Breach
High2018 (Fined 2019)Government / Land Registry

The Lands Authority's online application portal was misconfigured, leaving over 10GB of personal data — including identity cards and passport copies of 5,000+ applicants — publicly accessible and indexed by Google. Discovered by Times of Malta and The Shift News in November 2018. IDPC fined the Authority €5,000 in February 2019 — at the time Malta's highest GDPR fine. Portal was temporarily suspended.

Broader Threat Landscape
Context · 2025–2026
Details🎯 Scale of Attacks

PwC recorded over 6 million distinct attacks on Maltese IP ranges between August and October 2025 alone — more than one every second. Automated botnets scan every IP address indiscriminately.

Details🔒 SME Blind Spots

28.7% of Maltese enterprises reported a cyber incident in 2023 — well above the EU average of 21.5% (Eurostat 2024). And 20% still don't know whether they have been attacked.

Details🌐 iGaming as Attack Surface

Malta's iGaming sector generates over €714M GVA per half-year (H1 2025), 6.5% of GDP. Five of twelve incidents trace to iGaming — by far the highest-risk national sector.

Details⚠ Third-Party Risk Pattern

Fast Track, The Mill Adventure, and BOV were all compromised via trusted third-party platforms or supply-chain entry points. Vendor access management is now the single most critical control.

Details🏥 Healthcare Emerging Target

The Saint James Hospital attack (April 2025) is Malta's first confirmed healthcare cyberattack, consistent with an EU-wide trend of hospitals being targeted for ransom and medical record theft.

Details📋 544 Silent Breaches

544 personal data breach notifications were filed with the IDPC between GDPR implementation (May 2018) and October 2023. These are private notifications — the vast majority never appeared in any news source.
